# Cybersecurity Analyst Career Path

## Overview

Cybersecurity analysts are the practitioners who defend organizations from active threats on a day-to-day basis. The field encompasses a broad spectrum of roles — from entry-level Security Operations Center (SOC) analysts monitoring alerts, to senior threat hunters proactively searching for adversary activity, to incident responders containing live breaches. It is one of the most in-demand career paths in technology, with consistent job growth driven by escalating threat activity, regulatory requirements, and the expansion of digital attack surfaces.

San Diego's cybersecurity analyst market is anchored by the defense sector — the region hosts dozens of DoD contractors, naval installations (Naval Base San Diego, SPAWAR/PEO C4I), and intelligence-adjacent organizations. This creates strong demand for analysts with active DoD security clearances, familiarity with classified environments, and knowledge of military cybersecurity frameworks. Beyond defense, healthcare, telecom, and growing tech companies also fuel demand.

## SOC Tier 1 to Tier 2 to Tier 3

The Security Operations Center is the most common entry point for cybersecurity analysts, and the three-tier model provides a clear progression ladder.

**Tier 1 — Alert Triage Analyst**

Tier 1 analysts are the first responders to security alerts generated by SIEM platforms, EDR tools, and network monitoring systems. The role is high-volume and process-driven: follow the runbook, classify the alert, escalate or close. It is often described as a "phone bank for security events" and can be repetitive, but it builds critical pattern recognition skills.

Key activities:
- Monitoring SIEM dashboards (Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel, IBM QRadar) for triggered alerts
- Following investigation runbooks to triage potential threats
- Opening and escalating incident tickets
- Basic log analysis (Windows Event Logs, firewall logs, email headers)
- Shift work, including nights and weekends in 24x7 SOCs

Tier 1 is intentionally a learning environment. Analysts who progress fastest are those who read beyond the runbook, understand why each alert fires, and study for certifications on their own time.

**Tier 2 — Security Analyst / Incident Responder**

Tier 2 analysts handle escalations from Tier 1 and own the investigation and containment of confirmed incidents. They use deeper technical skills to trace attack chains, identify indicators of compromise, and execute containment actions.

Key activities:
- Full incident investigation from triage through containment and eradication
- Threat correlation across multiple data sources (endpoint, network, identity, email)
- Memory forensics and disk forensics for endpoint investigations
- Coordinating with IT teams on containment (isolating hosts, resetting credentials, blocking IOCs)
- Writing detailed incident reports and post-incident reviews
- Tuning SIEM detection rules to reduce false positives

**Tier 3 — Senior Analyst / Threat Hunter**

Tier 3 is where analysts stop waiting for alerts and start proactively hunting for threats that have evaded detection. Threat hunting assumes breach — the question is not "are we compromised?" but "where are the adversaries we haven't detected yet?"

Key activities:
- Developing and executing hypothesis-driven threat hunts using frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK
- Writing custom detection rules, SIEM queries (SPL for Splunk, KQL for Sentinel), and YARA rules
- Malware analysis and reverse engineering (basic to intermediate)
- Threat intelligence consumption and integration into detections
- Mentoring Tier 1 and Tier 2 analysts
- Red team / purple team collaboration to validate detection coverage

## Specialist Paths (Threat Intel, IR, Vulnerability)

After developing SOC fundamentals, many analysts specialize in one of several adjacent disciplines:

**Threat Intelligence Analyst:** Tracks adversary groups (APTs, cybercriminal organizations), analyzes TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures), and produces actionable intelligence for defensive teams and executive stakeholders. Strong writing skills required.

**Incident Response (IR) Specialist / DFIR:** Focuses on digital forensics and incident response, often for high-severity incidents. DFIR specialists may work in-house at large organizations or for consulting firms that respond to breaches at client sites.

**Vulnerability Management Analyst:** Runs vulnerability scanning programs (Tenable Nessus, Qualys, Rapid7), prioritizes findings using risk scoring (CVSS, EPSS), tracks remediation SLAs, and reports metrics to security leadership.

**Detection Engineer:** Translates threat intelligence and threat hunt findings into production detection logic. A hybrid role combining SOC experience with engineering skills.

## Key Certifications at Each Level

| Level | Certifications |
|-------|---------------|
| Tier 1 entry | CompTIA Security+, CompTIA A+/Network+ (pre-entry) |
| Tier 1-2 transition | CompTIA CySA+, EC-Council CEH, eJPT |
| Tier 2-3 | GIAC GCIH (Incident Handler), GIAC GCIA (Intrusion Analyst) |
| Specialist / Senior | GIAC GCFE/GCFA (Forensics), GIAC GREM (Malware), GIAC GCTI (Threat Intel), CISSP |
| Management track | CISM, CISSP, GIAC GSE (elite) |

CompTIA Security+ is the universally recognized baseline — DoD Directive 8570/8140 mandates it for many government and contractor roles, making it essential in San Diego's defense market.

## Essential Tools and Skills

**SIEM Platforms:** Splunk (SPL query language), Microsoft Sentinel (KQL), IBM QRadar, Elastic SIEM. Splunk and Sentinel proficiency are the most marketable in San Diego.

**EDR/XDR:** CrowdStrike Falcon, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, SentinelOne, Carbon Black. Understanding how endpoint detection differs from network-based detection is foundational.

**Network Analysis:** Wireshark, Zeek (Bro), network flow analysis. Ability to read packet captures is a differentiating skill.

**SOAR Platforms:** Palo Alto XSOAR, Splunk SOAR (Phantom), Microsoft Sentinel Playbooks. Automation skills are increasingly valuable.

**Frameworks:** MITRE ATT&CK (mandatory fluency), Cyber Kill Chain, Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis.

**Scripting:** Python for automating repetitive tasks, parsing logs, and interacting with APIs. PowerShell for Windows environments. Not required at Tier 1 but accelerates advancement significantly.

**Cloud:** AWS Security Hub, Azure Defender, GCP Security Command Center. Cloud investigation skills are now expected even in traditional enterprise SOC roles.

## Salary Ranges (San Diego / National)

Defense sector roles with clearances command meaningful premiums — a cleared Tier 2 analyst in San Diego earns significantly more than their uncleared equivalent. Contractors also often offer total package benefits (health, retirement matching, training budgets) that rival or exceed private sector employers.
