# CISM — Certified Information Security Manager

## Overview

The Certified Information Security Manager (CISM) is ISACA's management-level information security credential. Where CISSP tests broad technical and architectural security knowledge, CISM is squarely focused on security governance, program management, risk management, and incident response from a *management perspective*. It is the preferred credential for security professionals moving into or already in management roles — particularly those with their eyes on a CISO or VP of Security position.

The exam consists of 150 questions answered over 240 minutes (4 hours). Scoring is on a 200–800 scale, with a passing score of 450. Exam fees are $575 for ISACA members and $760 for non-members. Testing is available through PSI testing centers or remote proctoring.

CISM is consistently ranked in the top 5 highest-paying IT certifications globally. ISACA's 2024 salary survey reported average salaries for CISM holders exceeding $149,000 in North America.

## Who Should Get This

CISM is designed for:

- Information security managers and program leads who need to demonstrate governance and strategic thinking
- Senior security analysts or team leads targeting a management promotion
- IT risk managers who oversee security programs
- CISOs at small-to-mid-sized organizations who lack a formal credential
- Compliance officers with security oversight responsibilities
- Consultants advising on security program maturity

It is *not* ideal for hands-on technical practitioners (pen testers, security engineers, SOC analysts) who want to deepen technical skills — CISSP or CSX-P would serve them better. CISM's value is in communicating security as a business function, not in proving technical depth.

## Requirements

**Experience:** 5 years of professional information security management work experience. At least 3 of those 5 years must be in information security management (not just general IS/IT work). Waivers of up to 2 years are available:
- General information security experience (non-management) can substitute for up to 2 years of the non-management portion
- No waivers exist for the 3-year management experience requirement — that is fixed

**Passing the exam:** Must pass within 5 years of application.

**Application:** Submit within 5 years of passing the exam, verified by employer or supervisor signatures.

**Maintenance (CPE):** 120 CPE hours every 3-year reporting period, minimum 20 CPE per year. Annual maintenance fee applies.

## Exam Domains

The CISM exam covers 4 domains (2022 job practice update):

1. **Information Security Governance (17%)** — Establishing and maintaining a security governance framework aligned to business objectives; security strategy; organizational culture; roles and responsibilities; integration with enterprise risk management
2. **Information Security Risk Management (20%)** — Identifying, assessing, and managing information security risks; risk appetite and tolerance; risk treatment options; risk monitoring and communication
3. **Information Security Program (33%)** — The largest domain. Building, managing, and improving a security program; policies, standards, procedures; security architecture; metrics and reporting; resource management; security awareness and training
4. **Incident Management (30%)** — Incident response planning and execution; business continuity and disaster recovery coordination; post-incident review; forensics fundamentals; communication with stakeholders during incidents

The heavy weighting on domains 3 and 4 (63% combined) reflects CISM's focus on operational security management and organizational resilience rather than theory.

## Study Tips

- **ISACA QAE (Question, Answer and Explanation) Database** — Essential. The questions are scenario-based and heavily managerial in framing. Read explanations carefully — the "why" matters more than the answer itself.
- **ISACA CISM Review Manual** — Dense but authoritative. Use selectively; do not read cover to cover unless you are very new to security management.
- **CISM All-in-One Exam Guide (Peter Gregory, McGraw-Hill)** — One of the most accessible third-party study books. Well-structured for self-study.
- **ISACA online courses** — Available on the ISACA website; useful for Domain 1 (governance) if you have a purely technical background.
- Key mindset shift: CISM questions are written for a *manager*, not a technician. When choosing between answers, ask yourself: "What would a responsible security manager do first?" Answers that involve stakeholder communication, risk acceptance processes, and documented policies are frequently correct over purely technical fixes.
- Budget 80–120 hours study time for candidates already in security management roles.

## Career Impact

CISM is the single most recognized credential on a path to CISO:

- **Senior Security Analyst** → **Security Manager** (CISM signals readiness)
- **Security Manager** → **Director of Information Security** (CISM often required or strongly preferred)
- **Director** → **CISO / VP of Security** (CISM + experience is the standard combination at mid-market companies)

At enterprise companies, CISOs typically hold CISM, CISSP, or both. CISM is particularly valued in regulated industries: financial services, healthcare, government contracting, and insurance. It demonstrates that a security professional understands how to run a security program as a business function — budgeting, reporting to the board, managing vendors, and aligning security to business risk — not just how to deploy technical controls.

## How It Compares

| Cert | Focus | Best For | Management Weight |
|------|-------|----------|-------------------|
| CISM | Security governance & program mgmt | Managers, future CISOs | Very High |
| CISSP | Broad security domains | Architects, senior practitioners | Moderate |
| CISA | IT audit & assurance | Auditors | Low–Moderate |
| CRISC | IT risk & controls | Risk/GRC professionals | Moderate |

**CISM vs. CISSP:** This is the most common comparison. CISSP covers 8 broad security domains with significant technical depth (cryptography, software security, network architecture). CISM covers 4 domains entirely focused on management and governance. If you manage security teams and budgets: CISM first. If you design security architecture or advise on technical controls: CISSP first. Many senior security leaders ultimately earn both. CISSP tends to be more recognized in technical communities and vendor/consulting contexts; CISM is more recognized in corporate governance and board-level conversations.
