# CISSP — Certified Information Systems Security Professional

## Overview

The CISSP is the most globally recognized senior-level information security certification, issued by ISC2. It validates that a practitioner has the depth and breadth of knowledge needed to design, implement, and manage a best-in-class cybersecurity program. The credential is vendor-neutral and carries significant weight in hiring decisions across industries and government.

The exam uses Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) for English-language candidates: 100–150 questions delivered in 180 minutes. The CAT format adjusts question difficulty in real time based on your responses, which means the exam ends as soon as the algorithm has sufficient confidence in your competency level — either passing or failing. This makes "I had 100 questions" and "I had 150 questions" both valid passing outcomes; the number alone tells you nothing about your result. Non-English exams are linear, 250 questions, 360 minutes.

The exam fee is $749 USD.

## Who Should Get This

CISSP is aimed at experienced security professionals who are moving into or already operating in senior individual contributor or management roles. Ideal candidates include security managers, security architects, CISOs, IT directors with security responsibilities, and senior security engineers. It is not an entry-level certification — ISC2 requires demonstrated professional experience before you can hold the credential.

## Requirements

Candidates must have a minimum of five years of cumulative, paid, full-time work experience in two or more of the eight CISSP domains. A four-year college degree (or approved credential) waives one year of the experience requirement, reducing it to four years.

Candidates who pass the exam but do not yet meet the experience requirement become an Associate of ISC2 and have six years to accumulate the qualifying experience before upgrading to full CISSP status. This is the recommended path for strong candidates who are still building their career history.

After passing the exam, candidates must be endorsed by an active ISC2 member who can attest to their professional experience. ISC2 can act as the endorser if no suitable colleague is available.

Maintaining the credential requires earning 120 CPE (Continuing Professional Education) credits over the three-year renewal cycle and paying the annual AMF (Annual Maintenance Fee) of $135.

## Exam Domains

The eight CISSP Common Body of Knowledge (CBK) domains are:

1. Security and Risk Management (16%)
2. Asset Security (10%)
3. Security Architecture and Engineering (13%)
4. Communication and Network Security (13%)
5. Identity and Access Management (13%)
6. Security Assessment and Testing (12%)
7. Security Operations (13%)
8. Software Development Security (10%)

## Study Tips

The CISSP demands a management mindset, not just technical knowledge. Many technical candidates fail because they answer questions as an engineer rather than as a risk-aware security leader. When in doubt, select the answer that best protects the business — not the most technically complete solution.

Standard preparation timeline is 3–6 months for candidates with solid experience. Key resources include the official ISC2 CBK study guide, Shon Harris or Mike Chapple textbooks, and practice question banks (Boson, ISC2 official questions). Watch for "think like a manager" video series on YouTube — they are widely credited with shifting candidates into the correct mindset. After content review, focus heavily on practice exams under timed conditions.

## Career Impact

CISSP is often listed as a mandatory or strongly preferred credential in job postings for senior security roles across finance, healthcare, defense contracting, federal agencies, and consulting. It is approved under DoD Directive 8570/8140 for IAM Level III, the highest management level. Salary premiums associated with CISSP are consistently among the highest of any IT certification according to industry surveys.

## How It Compares

CISSP vs. CISM (ISACA): Both target senior professionals. CISM is more narrowly focused on security management and governance and is favored in GRC-heavy roles. CISSP covers a broader technical and management scope and tends to carry more weight in architecture and operations roles. Many senior practitioners hold both. If you are on a pure management track, evaluate CISM first; if you want maximum versatility, CISSP is the stronger choice.
