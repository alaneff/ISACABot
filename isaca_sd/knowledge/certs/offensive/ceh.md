# CEH — Certified Ethical Hacker

## Overview

The Certified Ethical Hacker (CEH) is an EC-Council credential that covers offensive security concepts including reconnaissance, scanning, exploitation, and post-exploitation techniques. It is one of the oldest and most name-recognized ethical hacking certifications, and it retains significant value in government, compliance-driven, and enterprise contexts where it is listed by name in policy documents or position requirements.

The exam consists of 125 multiple-choice questions completed within 240 minutes. The exam fee is approximately $550 USD for the exam voucher alone; the full EC-Council authorized training package is considerably more expensive. A practical exam version (CEH Practical) is also available as an optional add-on that tests hands-on skills in a lab environment.

## Who Should Get This

CEH is most appropriate for security professionals targeting government, military, or large enterprise positions where the credential is named explicitly in job requirements or compliance frameworks. It is recognized under DoD Directive 8570/8140 for CSSP Analyst and CSSP Infrastructure Support roles. Practitioners in roles that require demonstrated ethical hacking knowledge for compliance or audit purposes — rather than active red team engagements — also benefit from CEH.

## Requirements

EC-Council requires either two years of information security work experience or completion of an EC-Council official training program before a candidate can sit the exam. Candidates who do not meet the experience requirement must attend official training, which adds cost. The CEH must be renewed every three years by earning 120 ECE (EC-Council Continuing Education) credits and paying the renewal fee.

## Exam Domains

The CEH v13 curriculum covers 20 modules including:

- Footprinting and Reconnaissance
- Scanning Networks
- Enumeration
- Vulnerability Analysis
- System Hacking
- Malware Threats
- Sniffing and Social Engineering
- Session Hijacking
- Evading IDS, Firewalls, and Honeypots
- Web Application Hacking
- SQL Injection
- Cryptography
- Cloud, IoT, and OT Hacking

## Study Tips

CEH is primarily a knowledge-based exam, not a hands-on skills test. The multiple-choice format rewards memorization of tools, techniques, and their associated terminology as defined by EC-Council's curriculum. Study the official EC-Council courseware or Matt Walker's All-in-One CEH guide. Know the specific tools EC-Council associates with each attack phase — they ask about specific tool names frequently (e.g., Nmap for scanning, Metasploit for exploitation, Wireshark for sniffing).

Budget 2–3 months for preparation. Practice tests are critical given the question style and volume. Do not expect the exam to test you on the same depth of practical skill that the OSCP does — the CEH is a knowledge credential, and studying accordingly is the correct approach.

## Career Impact

In contexts where CEH appears in government contract requirements, position descriptions, or compliance checklists, it has concrete value and is worth pursuing. Federal government and defense contractor HR systems often filter resumes based on named certifications, and CEH appears often enough to justify the investment if your target market is that space.

In the practitioner community, CEH has a mixed reputation. Experienced penetration testers frequently note that the multiple-choice format does not validate hands-on capability, and the credential alone does not demonstrate that a candidate can actually execute an engagement. Employers hiring active red teamers and penetration testers increasingly favor hands-on credentials.

## How It Compares

CEH vs. OSCP: This is the most common comparison in the offensive security space, and the community is nearly unanimous that OSCP carries more weight for practitioners who want to demonstrate actual penetration testing ability. OSCP's 24-hour hands-on lab exam cannot be passed by memorization alone. OSCP holders are consistently preferred by penetration testing firms and mature security teams over CEH-only candidates. However, CEH is the compliance-friendly choice — many government and enterprise position descriptions list CEH but not OSCP. The pragmatic answer for practitioners: if you can invest the time and money, pursue OSCP. If a specific employer or contract requires CEH by name, add it to your portfolio.
