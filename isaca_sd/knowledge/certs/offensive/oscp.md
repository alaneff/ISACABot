# OSCP — Offensive Security Certified Professional

## Overview

The OSCP is the gold standard hands-on certification for penetration testers. Issued by Offensive Security (OffSec), it requires candidates to compromise machines in a live lab environment under a strict time constraint — making it one of the most respected and hardest-to-fake credentials in cybersecurity. Unlike nearly every other certification, OSCP cannot be passed by memorizing answers. You either compromise the boxes or you do not.

The exam is a 24-hour hands-on practical assessment. Candidates must exploit machines in an isolated lab network, document their findings, and submit a professional penetration test report within 24 hours after the exam ends. Scoring is based on points earned from successfully exploited machines plus report quality. The credential does not expire once earned, which is a meaningful differentiator in an industry where most certifications require periodic renewal.

## Who Should Get This

OSCP is appropriate for experienced IT or security practitioners who want to move into or advance within penetration testing and red team roles. It is not an entry-level credential — candidates with no hands-on scripting ability, no Linux command line comfort, and no prior exposure to hacking concepts will struggle severely. Ideal candidates are system administrators, network engineers, or security analysts with 2–5 years of hands-on experience who are serious about offensive security as a career direction.

## Requirements

The OSCP is obtained through OffSec's PEN-200 course (also called PWK — Penetration Testing with Kali Linux), which is a prerequisite for the exam attempt. The course and exam bundle starts at approximately $1,499 USD and includes lab access time (typically 90 days for base packages). Additional lab time can be purchased. There are no formal experience requirements enforced by OffSec, but candidates without a strong technical foundation are wasting their money.

Because OSCP does not expire, there is no renewal fee or CPE requirement.

## Exam Domains

PEN-200 and the OSCP exam cover the penetration testing methodology end-to-end:

- Information Gathering and Enumeration
- Vulnerability Scanning and Identification
- Exploitation (web applications, Windows, Linux, Active Directory)
- Active Directory Attacks (pivoting, lateral movement, privilege escalation)
- Post-Exploitation and Persistence
- Password Attacks and Cracking
- Client-Side Attacks
- Report Writing

## Study Tips

Completing the PEN-200 course material and lab exercises thoroughly is non-negotiable. Do not attempt the exam after only a few weeks in the labs. The community standard advice is to compromise at least 40–60 lab machines (including the harder challenge machines) before attempting the exam. Use the OffSec Proving Grounds Practice platform to supplement labs — it provides additional retired OSCP-style machines for individual practice.

TryHackMe and HackTheBox are excellent pre-study platforms to build foundational skills before starting PEN-200. The OSCP path on TryHackMe and the OSCP-like machine list maintained by the community on HackTheBox (tj_null's list) are well-regarded prep resources.

Realistic preparation time for candidates with solid IT experience is 3–6 months of consistent, focused study. Dedicated 2–4 hours per day of hands-on lab practice. The report matters — practice writing professional pentest reports before exam day. A failed exam attempt can be re-purchased and rescheduled; many candidates pass on the second attempt after refining their methodology.

## Career Impact

OSCP is the most employer-recognized hands-on offensive security credential globally. Penetration testing firms, red teams, and security consulting companies routinely list OSCP as a preferred or required qualification. Because it cannot be faked or memorized, it serves as a reliable filter for employers — an OSCP holder has demonstrably broken into machines under pressure. It commands salary premiums over CEH in offensive roles and is widely used as a shorthand for "this person can actually do the work."

## How It Compares

OSCP vs. CEH: OSCP is the practitioner choice; CEH is the compliance choice. Penetration testing firms and red teams hire OSCP holders. Government contracts and HR checklists list CEH. Many experienced practitioners hold both, obtaining CEH for compliance contexts while using OSCP to demonstrate actual capability to technical hiring managers.

OSCP vs. CISSP: These are not competing credentials — they serve entirely different roles. CISSP demonstrates broad security management knowledge; OSCP demonstrates hands-on offensive skill. A practitioner can and often does hold both on a senior red team or consulting path.
